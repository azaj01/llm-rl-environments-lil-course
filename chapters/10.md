# What we have learned and the future

## Our journey so far

Let's recap our exploration of Reinforcement Learning Environments so far.

In [chapter 1](01.md), we started by introducing classic Reinforcement Learning concepts (Agent, Environment) and seeing how to map them
into the Language Models domain.

In [chapter 2](02.md), we introduced Verifiers, an open-source library to build RL environments as software artifacts, and saw
examples of single-turn and multi-turn environments.

In [chapter 3](03.md), things got real. We developed a RL environment to evaluate and train our Language Models on Tic Tac Toe.
We started simple and then refined an environment that was robust enough for effective RL training.

In [chapter 4](04.md), we used the Tic Tac Toe environment to evaluate `openai/gpt-5-mini`, a good proprietary model and `LiquidAI/LFM2-2.6B`,
a small but skilled open model. We noticed a significant capability gap that we wanted to close using RL.

Realizing that part of this gap derived from the small model's inability to properly follow our required format, we decided to do a short Supervised Fine-Tuning run. In [chapter 5](05.md), we performed synthetic data generation, using the Tic Tac Toe environment to sample 200 games with `openai/gpt-5-mini`.

In [chapter 6](06.md), we used this data to perform SFT using PRIME-RL and got a model that followed the format well. 
It was not yet a decent player, but the stage was set for Reinforcement Learning.

In [chapter 7](07.md), we did a quick recap of GRPO (and CISPO), a very popular algorithm for RL on Language Models, and finally RL-tuned
our model using Verifiers' RLTrainer. We discussed the most important parameters to set to ensure stable training and optimize GPU memory usage. At this point, the trained model was competent but not perfect. We weren't done yet.

In [chapter 8](08.md), we tried to squeeze the last drops of performance out of RL training. By training against harder opponents and increasing the temperature to force exploration, the model reached mastery and even beat `openai/gpt-5-mini`!

In [chapter 9](09.md), I went over the failed experiments I ran during the process of getting our model to learn Tic Tac Toe, hopefully sharing something useful for your own runs.

And that brings us to the end of our Tic Tac Toe project. Looking back, we took a small model that couldn't even format a move properly and transformed it into a master of the game.

## What's next?

While this is the end of the course (at least for now), you can keep autonomously exploring this domain.

- To build a stronger foundation, start with the resources suggested in [chapter 1](01.md).
- To figure out what environments people build, go to the [Environments Hub](https://app.primeintellect.ai/dashboard/environments) and
start exploring. A while ago, I also wrote an [article](https://huggingface.co/blog/anakin87/environments-hub) introducing this community space for sharing environments (some parts of the article might not be up-to-date).
- It's also worth taking a look at [OpenEnv](https://meta-pytorch.org/OpenEnv), another framework to build RL Environments
  for LLMs, supported by Meta and Hugging Face.
- **And what to build next?** Something I'm very excited about is this: train a small Language Model on 2-3 tools you often
use and try to beat a large proprietary model in that specific task. One recommended resource is the [Wiki Search example](https://github.com/PrimeIntellect-ai/prime-rl/tree/main/examples/wiki_search) in the PRIME-RL repository.

## Final words

I hope you leave this course finding Reinforcement Learning Environments as fascinating as I do.

Looking back, we didn't teach the model Tic Tac Toe by showing it how to play. We gave it a space to play, guided its learning
through rewards, and watched it improve.

Nowadays, Reinforcement Learning complements Supervised Fine-Tuning in Language Model post-training. 
You can do this at home, too. If you can define a clear reward signal, you can build an environment and train a small, specialized model to beat a large proprietary model on a specific task, at a fraction of the cost.

If you spot any errors in this course, let me know by opening a GitHub issue.
Feel free to follow me on [my social profiles](../README.md#course-author), share what you build and keep tinkering!
