# What did not work: a Tic Tac Toe Post-Mortem

Over this course, we have seen how to go from a model that rarely outuputs valid Tic Tac Toe moves to an excellent
player, practically unbeatable.

But to achieve this result, I underwent several failed experiments, so I though it can be interesting to discuss and
learn from them.

## A 0.6B model that plays Tic Tac Toe perfectly, OR NOT?

I started with the same goal in mind: pick a small open model and transform it into a Tic Tac Toe master with
RL Environments, but I made different initial decisions.

### Initial choices

The environment was a bit simpler. First, opponent skill was not much tunable: half of the times, the model played
 against a random opponent and other half of the times against a perfect player.

Remembering my child Tic Tac Toe matches, I thought: playing at the center is a good starting move. So when the opponent
was optimal, I made it always start from the center.

For the **Minimax algorithm** (optimal strategy), I thought: this is an implementation detail, let's have Claude code it and 
I ended up with something similar to this:

```python
def minimax(board: list[str | None], is_maximizing: bool) -> tuple[float, int | None]:
    """Minimax algorithm. Returns (score, best_move) for the current player."""
    
    # ...
    
    free = get_free_positions(board=board)
    if not free:
        return 0, None

    player = "O" if is_maximizing else "X"
    best_score = -float("inf") if is_maximizing else float("inf")
    best_move = free[0]

    for pos in free:
        board[pos] = player
        score, _ = minimax(board=board, is_maximizing=not is_maximizing)
        board[pos] = None

        if is_maximizing and score > best_score or not is_maximizing and score < best_score:
            best_score, best_move = score, pos

    return best_score, best_move
```

It's not immmediately understandable, but this algorithm has a bias towards the first free optimal position on the board:
in case multiple moves have equal optimal score, the first free position (ranging from 0 to 8) is always selected.

For models, I had the ambitious goal of using very small models. I thought **Qwen-3-0.6B** was perfect: very small size,
good capabilities, already able to produce reasoning, non-zero rewards on my evaluation runs.

One aspect I immediately found out: reasoning models tend to produce long thinking traces; for Qwen-3-0.6B, more than
400 tokens per completion which is a lot for Tic Tac Toe. But who cares? This will just make training longer and worst
case we truncate overly long completions

Let's talk about **batch size**: as I stressed over the course, it's important to have large values, meaning that the model
learns from a sufficient number of different games, especially if the environment is diverse. But I learnt this
after failed experiments. Despite Verifiers docs suggest values in the 256-1024 range, this means a training where you
see slower progress, so I initally set it to 64: my model learnt from 64 games at once starting from 8 initial positions
and skills (GRPO Group size = 8).

### Results and lessons learned

Qwen3-0.6B had 2% draws against an optimal opponent; 40 wins, 6 draws and 14 losses against a random opponent.

Using the configuration described above, I trained it with Reinforcement Learning.

Results were good: 99% draws against an optimal opponent; 78 wins, 6 draws and 16 losses against a random opponent.

Ok, the fact that our model was stronger against a perfect players than vs a random one sounded suspicious but in any
case I was happy with my experiment...

Until I actually tried to play myself against the trained model (using the
HF space I shared previously): the model was clueless and bad. What a slap in my face. How is it possible?

It turns out that much is due to the Minimax implementation I overlooked: if the perfect player always starts from the
center and is biased towards the first free optimal position, 
**I was training my small model against a specific type of optimal player!**
And over ~32K games against it, Qwen simply memorized it. This also explains why performance is
higher vs the "perfect" player than the random player.

How to fix? Do not start from the center by default and, above all, randomly choose one of the optimal moves, not the 
first one.

Now with the next one: training alredy reasoning models on a specific task is acceptable but, as we said, they tend
to output long thinking traces (~500 tokens for this model, >1000 tokens for bigger models in the Qwen 3 release).

In my case, with limited GPU resources and time, I ended up truncating most of longer completions at the beginning to fit my shorter
limits. This means that we use a part of the training budget to just teach the model to respond concisely; at the same time,
there is a risk of "lobotomization": we can basically defeat the purpose of the original training of the model to produce
long Chains of Toughts.

Practical takeaway: if your task is scoped and GPU resources are highly constrained, 
**start from an instruct model and transform it into a reasoning model for your task**. This produces shorter training runs, a more concise but not "lobotimized" model.

After my initial experiment, I keep trying to teach Tic Tac Toe to very small models (< 1.5B parameters). 
What I found is that, while Reinforcement Learning is effective to improve them, it seems hard to push them to real
competency. By inspecting their completions, it seems that they lack spatial understanding capabilities, they 
struggle to make a sense of the board. For example, Qwen-3-0.6B really struggled to "see" diagonal lines.

What I recommend: in addition to evaluating the base model on your environment, 
**look at a few completions and choose a model that exhibits promising behaviors** that can be reinforced, even if the current results are not satisfying. I did this with `LiquidAI/LFM2-2.6B` and got good performance after training.
